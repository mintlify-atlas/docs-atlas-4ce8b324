The Fix phase applies one targeted change at a time ŌĆö one named cause, one skill, one PR. The discipline matters: a PR that simultaneously wraps four data classes in `ImmutableList`, migrates two custom modifiers to `Modifier.Node`, and moves three state reads down a phase produces a Macrobenchmark delta that cannot be attributed to any single change. If startup improved but scroll regressed, you cannot bisect the cause. Keep each fix minimal, re-measure after it lands, and only then pick the next issue from your diagnosis list.

## The principle

Every fix skill in the library is scoped to one cause. The skill's prerequisites list what the Diagnose phase must have told you before you apply it, and its Verification checklist confirms the fix landed without introducing a new regression. Read both before writing code.

<Tip>
After every fix, return to the Measure phase and re-run the same Macrobenchmark before applying the next fix. A fix that improves P90 frame timing may shift the bottleneck elsewhere; a second fix applied without re-measuring produces an unfalsifiable claim.
</Tip>

## What each fix skill expects as input

Every fix skill lists prerequisites in its **When to use** section. The most common expectation is a specific line from `composables.txt` or `classes.txt` naming the cause. Before opening a fix skill, confirm you have:

- The composable function name and the unstable parameter (from `composables.txt`)
- The root cause of the instability (a `var` field, a `List`, a third-party type) from `classes.txt`
- A Macrobenchmark number from the Measure phase to compare against after the fix

Without those inputs, the fix skill will tell you to go back and diagnose first.

<Note>
Skippability is a diagnostic output of the fix, not a target. Do not apply stability fixes to composables that are not on the hot path ŌĆö a `@Preview` composable or a settings screen that renders once never benefits from being skippable, and the annotation introduces a contract you must maintain indefinitely.
</Note>
